This document provides a comprehensive overview of the four primary diagnostic and screening methods used to detect active tuberculosis.

## 1. Rapid Molecular Assays (e.g., GeneXpert)

* **Time to Result:** Less than 2 hours.
* **How it works:** It uses an automated polymerase chain reaction (PCR) to amplify and detect the specific DNA of the *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* bacteria directly from a patient's sample.
* **The X-Factor (Drug Resistance & Swabs):** It does not just detect the presence of TB; it simultaneously detects genetic mutations that cause resistance to Rifampicin (the most critical first-line TB drug). Discovering a patient has drug-resistant TB on day one completely changes the trajectory of their treatment. Additionally, updated WHO guidelines (as of 2026) support running these molecular tests using simple **tongue swabs** instead of deep-lung sputum, a massive breakthrough for children and severely ill adults.

## 2. Sputum Smear Microscopy

* **Cost:** Extremely low (often under $2–$3 per test).
* **How it works:** A century-old technique where a sputum sample is smeared on a glass slide, dyed with a specific stain (like Ziehl-Neelsen), and examined under a standard light microscope by a technician looking for the physical bacteria.
* **The X-Factor (Accessibility):** It requires no consistent electricity for automated machines, no expensive proprietary test cartridges, and very basic laboratory infrastructure. It remains the frontline diagnostic tool in many rural and severely low-resource settings.
* **The Downside:** It has terrible sensitivity. It requires a massive bacterial load in the lungs to be visible to the human eye. Because of this, it frequently misses active TB, especially in children and individuals co-infected with HIV.

## 3. Mycobacterial Culture

* **Accuracy:** The definitive "Gold Standard." Sensitivity is nearly 100%, and specificity is absolute. 
* **How it works:** The sputum sample is placed in a specialized liquid or solid nutrient medium in a highly controlled lab environment. If the bacteria are present, they will eventually grow and multiply, proving active disease beyond a shadow of a doubt.
* **The X-Factor (Comprehensive Profiling):** Once the bacteria are grown in the dish, technicians can physically expose them to every known TB drug to create a complete "drug susceptibility profile." This is the only way to know with absolute certainty which combination of antibiotics will cure a complex, multi-drug resistant case.
* **The Downside (Extreme Delay):** TB bacteria replicate at a glacial pace compared to standard bacteria. A culture takes anywhere from **2 to 6 weeks** to yield a final result, meaning doctors cannot wait for this test to come back before they begin treating a highly suspected case.

## 4. Chest X-Ray (with AI/CAD)

* **Accuracy:** Extremely high sensitivity, but very low specificity. (An X-ray cannot definitively diagnose TB, as the lung damage looks identical to pneumonia, fungal infections, or cancer). It is used as a **Triage and Screening Tool**.
* **How it works:** Captures images of the lungs to show physical damage, cavities, fluid, or scarring caused by an infection.
* **The X-Factor (AI & CAD):** The traditional bottleneck for chest X-rays is that they require a scarce, trained radiologist to interpret the shadows. The WHO now formally recommends using Computer-Aided Detection (CAD) software. These AI machine-learning algorithms analyze the image in seconds and output a "TB probability score." This allows mobile clinics to screen hundreds of people a day, instantly identifying who needs to provide a sample for a GeneXpert test, massively streamlining the diagnostic pipeline.
